Angkor Wat in Krong Siem Reap, Cambodia

  1. Home
  2. Indianization

Posted on April 14th, 2021 at 5:00 PM

Angkor Wat is the largest religious structure (temple complex) in the world by land area of about 402 acres. It was originally constructed as a personal mausoleum for King Suryavarman II, and dedicated to Vishnu in the early 12th century. It is one of the best preserved temples in the world, and when buddhism arrived it was converted into a Buddhist temple. Today it is has become of the most important pilgrimage sites for both Buddhism and Hinduism.

The building has extensive decorations, mostly in the form of bas-relief friezes. Many of the large scale scenes are mainly depicting episodes from Hindu epics of the Ramayana and the Mahabharata. One of the most celebrated scenes is the Churning of the Sea of Milk, showing 92 Asuras and 88 Devas using the serpent Vasuki to churn the sea under Vishnu's direction.

The galleries are all exact lengths of 202.14 meters long. While the eastern corridors are 114.22 or 114.24 meters long. I believe that this is significant because having multiple buildings be this accurate is near impossible in their time. What’s more surprising is that they used cubit lengths to measure this all out. A cubit length is the distance between the elbows to the most outstretched fingertips. From the definition given, it’s clear to see that this is not the most accurate way to measure as different people will have differing cubit lengths. Since it’s so different, it’s astounding how all of them can be identical or near identical being off by only 0.02 meter (which by the way is less than an inch off). A standard cubit in Cambodia between 0.4 meters and 0.5 meters which is more than 3 inches. For a massive structure to have an error of less than inch which is less than 0.01% of the length of the total structure, to me is incredible.

Angkor Wat is of such extraordinary construction that it is not possible to describe it with a pen, particularly since it is like no other building in the world. It has towers and decoration and all the refinements which the human genius can conceive of.

António da Madalena, 1586

Angkor Wat represents a lot of Indian culture in terms of architecture because of religious influence. Angkor Wat depicts countless Hindu portrayals, and also does a great job at connecting the temple to a more celestial purpose. The exact measurements of this temple, replicates the spatial universe in miniature scale. Unlike other architectural styles, Indian temple architecture is due to the towers and inscriptions within the temple concerning hindu beliefs. Another great indicator of temple architecture is the Garbha Griha, and Mandapas. While Angkor Wat does not have prominent Garbha Griha, the towering patterns symbolizing the place where the Gods live, is a clear indication of how Angkor Wat embodies more Indian Architecture.
