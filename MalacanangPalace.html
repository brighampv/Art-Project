Malacañang Palace in Manila, Philippines

  1. Home
  2. Westernization

Posted on April 14th, 2021 at 5:00 PM

Malacañang Palace is the official residence and principal workplace of the President of the Philippines. It is located in old Manila district of San Miguel, and is commonly associated with Mendiola Street. The term "Malacañang" is often used as a metonym for the president and his advisers. The sprawling Malacañang Palace complex includes numerous mansions and office buildings designed and built largely in bahay na bato and neoclassical style

Bahay na Bato architecture is type of architectural style developed in the Philippines' colonial period. This architectural style consist of stone bricks as the foundation, tiled or thatch roof to give flexibility during earthquakes, and windows and openings for open ventilation. Neoclassical style on the other hand originated from Italy and France and can be characterized by symmetry, simple geometry, and proportionality either as a grid system or in abstract geometry.

From the pictures above it is clear to see the Bahay na Bato architecture in the tiled roofs as well as the windows and openings to provide greater ventilation in the building. Because Filipinos were influenced by western cultures, they aspired to become more like their colonizers. Adding styles that were deemed luxurious by their oppressors, from this belief, many well respected buildings in the Philippines were built with European preferences in mind, such as the Neoclassical architectural style.

When the Philippines came under American sovereignty following the Spanish–American War, Malacañang Palace became the residence of the American Governor-General, starting with General Wesley Merritt, the first American Military Governor to reside at the Palace in 1898. William Howard Taft became the first Civil Governor to reside there in 1901. They and subsequent Governors-General continued to improve and enlarge the Palace, buying more land and reclaiming more of the Pasig River. Left and right wings were added. An azotea facing the river was joined to the existing azotea. A staircase was transferred to the center of the foyer with galleries built around the stairway where the public could circulate during crowded receptions. The ground floor was raised above the flood line, replacing wood with concrete, and beautifying the interiors with hardwood panelling and adding magnificent chandeliers.

Malacañang Palace survived the Second World War, the only major government building left standing after the Bombing of Manila. By the time former President Diosdado Macapagal moved to the palace, it was in need of immense repair and restoration works. His wife, Mrs. Eva Macapagal, initiated a massive beautification project which drove sidewalk vendors away from the grounds and turned muddy areas to landscaped gardens.
